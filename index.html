Greenhouse Gas Emissions · From Global View to Asian Subregions

When exploring the data on how industries and regions contribute to global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from 2010 to 2023, one pattern stood out to us immediately: Asia leads all continents across every major gas type.

This was a consistent trend that appeared across our dataset, so we decided to dig deeper and use two external sources for context. The two articles we found — the United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific’s 2024 climate review and the “Emerging Carbon Dioxide Hotspots in East Asia” Nature article — helped us better understand why Asia shows such high emission levels and how our visual findings match broader research.

Global Gas Emissions by Continent: Asia Leads Across All Gas Types

Our first graph gives a global snapshot of how emissions differ across continents. The format highlights how Asia’s proportional difference across regions is not just limited to carbon dioxide (CO₂); it also extends across methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases. This aligns with the UN ESCAP 2024 review, which estimates that Asia/Pacific alone accounts for nearly 60% of global emissions in recent years. The article attributes this to rapid industrialization, large population growth, and heavy reliance on energy-intensive sectors.

Graph interactive cue: Hover over each continent to compare how the distribution of gas types shifts globally and see which gases contributed the most to Asia’s lead.

Asian Subregions · Industry Composition (Stacked Bars)

Once we noticed Asia’s global lead, we zoomed in using a stacked bar chart to break emissions down by Asian subregions. This layered breakdown makes it easier to compare industries like electricity and heat generation, manufacturing, agriculture, transportation, and construction across Eastern, Southern, Western, and Southeastern Asia.

The graph shows that Eastern Asia has a very large emission contribution from electricity and manufacturing, while other regions show more influence from agriculture or transportation. In the Nature study on East Asian carbon hotspots, the authors show that China (in Eastern Asia) produces the majority of industrial and energy-related emissions, which matches what we see in our visualization.

Graph interactive cue: Hover over Southern or Western Asia to see how industrial activity differs from Eastern Asia.

Emissions Hotspots · Industries × Asian Subregions (Greenhouse Gas)

The third visualization brings everything together by highlighting where the most intense emission hotspots occur, displaying both industry and subregions simultaneously. The heatmap makes it easy to identify these “hotspots” because the highest-emitting combinations visually stand out through darker colors.

As we suspected, Eastern Asia’s electricity/energy and manufacturing sectors light up as the most intense hotspots, directly reflecting the pattern in the Nature CO₂ hotspot study.

Graph interactive cue: Hover over different cells to compare which industries dominate each Asian subregion in the selected year.

Conclusions and Next Steps

Together, these three visualizations show a clear narrative: Asia dominates global greenhouse gas emissions, and within Asia, certain industries and subregions are responsible for the majority of output.

This raises important questions for climate policy, especially around how industrial transitions should be supported and how responsibility should be shared across regions. One could also say that it highlights inequality in both emissions and climate impacts.

As a next step, it would be interesting to map emissions at a more granular geographic level or to visualize projections to see how these hotspots might change in the future.
